Antifungal Drugs

Fungal infections of the nails ?
✓ Tinea unguium (onychomycosis)
Fungal Infection of the Feet ?
✓ Tinea pedis (athlete's foot)
Fungal infection of the Head, Ma ring worms filya ?
✓ Tinea capitis
Fungal infection of the body, in the form of ringworms also
✓ Tinea corporis
Fungal Infection of the buttock Jock itch ?
✓ Tinea cruris
What symptoms are common to all these fungal infections?
✓ Pruritus (itching) and erythema (redness)
How do ringworm infections typically present?
✓ Annular (ring-shaped) scaling rash with a clear center
What is the most common cause of yeast infections?
✓ Candida albicans and other Candida species
What are common manifestations of Candida infections?
✓ Oral thrush, vaginal candidiasis, axilla/groin/gluteal fold infections, diaper rash in children
What causes Pityriasis versicolor (Tinea versicolor)?
✓ Malassezia furfur (Pityrosporum ovale)
How does Pityriasis versicolor present?
✓ Hypopigmented and hyperpigmented macules, mostly on the
What is the main target for many antifungal drugs?
✓ The fungal cell membrane
What sterol is found in fungal cell membranes?
✓ Ergosterol
What sterol is found in mammalian cell membranes?
✓ Cholesterol
Why are antifungal drugs selectively toxic to fungi?
✓ Because fungi have ergosterol in their membranes, while mammals have cholesterol
How do some antifungal drugs affect the fungal cell membrane?
✓ They bind to ergosterol and increase membrane permeability
How do other antifungal drugs target ergosterol?
✓ They inhibit the synthesis of ergosterol
What is the main mechanism of action for most antifungal drugs?
✓ Inhibition of ergosterol synthesis or binding
What are the main classes of antifungal drugs?
✓ Polyenes, Allylamines, Echinocandins, Azoles, Others
Name some Polyene antifungal drugs.
✓ Amphotericin B, Natamycin, Nystatin
Name some Allylamine antifungal drugs.
✓ Terbinafine, Naftifine
Name an Echinocandin antifungal drug.
✓ Caspofungin
What are the two main types of Azole derivatives?
✓ Imidazoles (Diazoles) and Triazoles
Name some Imidazole (Diazole) antifungal drugs.
✓ Clotrimazole, Econazole, Ketoconazole, Miconazole, Tioconazole
Name some Triazole antifungal drugs.
✓ Fluconazole, Itraconazole, Voriconazole, Posaconazole
Name some other antifungal drugs.
✓ Griseofulvin, Flucytosine, Tolnaftate, Ciclopirox
Which polyene drug is used for systemic and subcutaneous mycoses?
✓ Amphotericin B
Which polyene drugs are limited to topical use for superficial mycoses?
✓ Nystatin and Natamycin
What is the mechanism of action of polyene antifungal drugs?
✓ They selectively bind to ergosterol in the fungal cell membrane
What happens when polyenes bind to ergosterol?
✓ Membrane permeability increases, causing leakage of cytoplasmic contents
Is Amphotericin B absorbed from the gut?
✓ No, it is not absorbed from the gut and is available as a parenteral preparation
Is Amphotericin B available for topical use?
✓ Yes, it is available in topical preparations for superficial infections
What determines the dose and route of Amphotericin B administration?
✓ The site and severity of the infection
When are higher doses of Amphotericin B used?
✓ For infections caused by resistant fungi like Aspergillus species
When are lower doses of Amphotericin B used?
✓ For esophageal and urinary tract infections
What are the main indications for Amphotericin B?
✓ Active against a wide variety of fungal infections, including cryptococcal meningitis and histoplasmosis
Why is Amphotericin B called "Ampho-terrible"?
✓ It is considered the most toxic antimicrobial drug in use today
What are common gastrointestinal adverse effects of Amphotericin B?
✓ Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (when given parenterally)
What renal toxicity can Amphotericin B cause?
✓ Renal toxicity leading to hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia
What cardiotoxic effects are associated with Amphotericin B?
✓ Arrhythmias and blood pressure changes
What neurological disorders can Amphotericin B cause?
✓ Hearing loss, convulsions, diplopia, peripheral neuropathy, encephalopathy
What other adverse effects are seen with Amphotericin B?
✓ Liver dysfunction, rash, thrombophlebitis at the site of injection
What are the lipid formulations of Amphotericin B?
✓ Amphotericin cholesteryl sulphate (AMPHOTEC), Amphotericin phospholipid complex (ABELCET), Amphotericin liposomal complex (AMBISOME)
Why are lipid formulations of Amphotericin B preferred in some patients?
✓ They have better pharmacokinetic properties, reduce renal toxicity, and are preferred in renal impairment or intolerance to traditional formulation
What happens after administration of lipid formulations of Amphotericin B?
✓ The drug is sequestered in liver and spleen cells, then slowly released over several days to maintain adequate plasma concentration for treatment
What is Nystatin active against?
✓ Candida species
What topical preparations of Nystatin are available?
✓ Creams, ointments, and powders for mucocutaneous candidiasis
How is Nystatin used for oral and intestinal candidiasis?
✓ Orally administered tablets and suspensions
How is Nystatin used for vaginal candidiasis?
✓ Vaginal pessaries
What is Natamycin active against?
✓ Aspergillus, Candida, Fusarium, and Penicillium species
What is the main preparation of Natamycin?
✓ Ophthalmic suspension for fungal blepharitis, conjunctivitis, or keratitis
What are azole derivatives used for?
✓ Synthetic drugs used to treat various mycoses
How are azoles used for superficial vs. systemic infections?
✓ Some are applied topically for superficial infections; others are administered for systemic infections like onychomycosis
How are azole derivatives for systemic infections administered?
✓ Several are given orally; fluconazole and voriconazole can also be given intravenously
What is required for absorption of ketoconazole, itraconazole, and posaconazole?
✓ Gastric acid is required; drugs that reduce gastric acid should not be given concurrently
How are azole derivatives metabolized and excreted?
✓ They undergo hepatic biotransformation; parent compound and metabolites are excreted in urine and stool
What is the mechanism of action of azole derivatives?
✓ They block the biosynthesis of ergosterol by inhibiting 14-demethylase (a cytochrome P450 enzyme)
What happens when ergosterol synthesis is inhibited by azoles?
✓ Cell plasma membrane formation is blocked or impeded
Are azole derivatives fungistatic or fungicidal?
✓ They can be either fungistatic or fungicidal, depending on the organism and drug concentration
What is the spectrum of activity for azole derivatives?
✓ Active against a wide range of fungi; alternatives to Amphotericin B for many systemic and subcutaneous infections
What is itraconazole particularly useful for?
✓ Treatment of blastomycosis, histoplasmosis, and onychomycosis (nail infections)
Why is fluconazole used for cryptococcal meningitis?
✓ It achieves better cerebrospinal fluid concentration and is used for treatment and prevention after Amphotericin B
What other infections is fluconazole used to treat?
✓ Mucocutaneous infections (oral, pharyngeal, esophageal candidiasis), disseminated candidiasis (endocardial), and vaginal candidiasis (single dose)
Why are voriconazole and posaconazole called second generation triazoles?
✓ They have enhanced activity against Aspergillus and Candida species
How potent is voriconazole compared to fluconazole?
✓ Voriconazole is 60 to 100 times more potent than fluconazole and effective against non-albicans Candida and Aspergillus fumigatus
What are the formulations and uses of ketoconazole?
✓ Available in oral and topical forms; oral use is limited, topical is useful for seborrheic dermatitis
What are the uses of clotrimazole?
✓ Topical treatment for candida infections of mouth, throat, vagina, tinea versicolor, tinea pedis, and tinea cruris
