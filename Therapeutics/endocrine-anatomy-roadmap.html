1. Pituitary Gland (Hypophysis)

Overview: The pituitary gland, also known as the hypophysis, is a small, pea-sized endocrine gland located inferior to the hypothalamus within the sella turcica of the sphenoid bone. Embryologically, it has two distinct origins:

  • The adenohypophysis (anterior pituitary) and pars intermedia are derived from oral ectoderm.
  • The neurohypophysis (posterior pituitary) is an outgrowth of the hypothalamus.

Functionally, structurally, and embryologically, the pituitary gland is intimately connected to the hypothalamus, forming the hypothalamic-hypophyseal system.

Adenohypophysis Includes:

  • Pars distalis (main part, ~75%)
  • Pars tuberalis (wraps around the infundibulum)
  • Pars intermedia (between anterior and posterior pituitary)

Histological Cell Types:

  • Chromophils: Secretory cells with visible cytoplasmic granules, further divided into:
    • Acidophils:
      • Somatotrophs → secrete growth hormone (GH)
      • Lactotrophs/Mammotrophs → secrete prolactin (PRL)
    • Basophils:
      • Corticotrophs → secrete ACTH and β-LPH from POMC
      • Thyrotrophs → secrete TSH
      • Gonadotrophs → secrete FSH and LH
  • Chromophobes: Weakly staining; thought to be degranulated chromophils, progenitor, or stem cells.

Pars Distalis Function:

  • Regulates endocrine glands (thyroid, adrenal, gonads)
  • Controls milk production
  • Influences growth and metabolism
  • Gonadotrophs secrete both FSH and LH
  • Corticotrophs produce POMC, cleaved into ACTH and β-LPH

Pars Tuberalis: Surrounds the infundibular stalk and consists mostly of gonadotrophs. Its precise function is unknown.

Pars Intermedia: Located between the pars distalis and neurohypophysis; active during fetal life. Produces:

  • Melanocyte-Stimulating Hormones (MSH)
  • γ-LPH
  • β-endorphin

Its functional role in adults remains unclear.

Neurohypophysis (Posterior Pituitary):

  • Unmyelinated axons of neurosecretory cells (from supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei)
  • Pituicytes: Glial-like supporting cells
  • Stores and releases:
    • ADH — from supraoptic nucleus
    • Oxytocin — from paraventricular nucleus

Hormones travel via the hypothalamic-hypophyseal tract and are released into the bloodstream.

Blood Supply & Portal System:

  • Superior Hypophyseal Arteries:
    • Supply the median eminence and infundibular stalk
    • Form the primary capillary plexus (fenestrated)
    • Capillaries recombine into portal venules draining into the secondary capillary plexus of the adenohypophysis
  • Inferior Hypophyseal Arteries: Primarily supply the neurohypophysis

Together, they form the hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal system, essential for transporting regulatory neuropeptides to the anterior pituitary.


16. Thyroid Gland

Location & Structure: The thyroid gland is located anterior to the larynx in the neck. It is composed of two lobes surrounded by a dense irregular collagenous connective tissue capsule. Septa extend into the gland, dividing it into lobules and carrying blood vessels, lymphatics, and nerves.

Origin: The thyroid originates embryologically from the foregut endoderm near the base of the developing tongue.

Follicle Structure:

  • Thyroid follicles are the structural and functional units of the gland.
  • Each follicle is lined by simple cuboidal epithelium and has a central colloid-filled lumen.
  • Follicles are separated by sparse reticular connective tissue and well-vascularized capillaries.
  • The colloid contains thyroglobulin — precursor to thyroid hormones.
  • The thyroid stores large amounts of secretion extracellularly in the follicular lumen.

Follicular (Principal) Cells (Thyrocytes):

  • Light Microscopy (L/M):
    • Simple cuboidal cells
    • Round nucleus with prominent nucleoli
    • Basophilic cytoplasm
    • Apical surface reaches follicular lumen
  • Electron Microscopy (E/M):
    • Numerous mitochondria
    • Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum (RER)
    • Supranuclear Golgi Complex
    • Apically located lysosomes
    • Small vesicles with newly formed thyroglobulin
    • Apical short microvilli
  • Function: Secrete T₃ and T₄ which regulate basal metabolic rate. Activity controlled by TSH from the anterior pituitary. Shape indicates activity (low columnar = active; squamous = hypoactive).

Parafollicular Cells (Clear Cells / C-Cells):

  • Location: Found within the basal lamina of the follicular epithelium or between follicles.
  • Light Microscopy (L/M):
    • Pale-stained (clear) cells
    • Found singly or in clusters
    • Do not reach the follicular lumen
    • Larger than follicular cells (2–3 times)
    • Only 0.1% of epithelial population
    • Round nucleus
  • Electron Microscopy (E/M):
    • Mitochondria
    • Moderate RER
    • Well-developed Golgi Complex
    • Small granules (100–180 nm) containing calcitonin
  • Function: Secrete calcitonin in response to elevated blood Ca²⁺. Inhibits osteoclast activity. Less critical than parathyroid hormone or vitamin D in calcium regulation.

2. Beta Blockers

Examples: Carvedilol, Bisoprolol, Metoprolol succinate

Mechanism: Block β1-adrenergic receptors → reduce sympathetic overactivity

Important: Start at low dose and titrate slowly in stable patients

Key Points:

  • Only use evidence-based beta blockers in HF
  • May initially worsen symptoms before improvement
  • Contraindicated in acute decompensated HF

3. Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists

Examples: Spironolactone, Eplerenone

Mechanism: Block aldosterone effects → reduce sodium retention and fibrosis

Monitoring Required: Potassium levels (risk of hyperkalemia), renal function

Clinical Use:

  • NYHA Class II-IV with reduced EF
  • Post-MI with reduced EF and symptoms

4. ARNI (Sacubitril/Valsartan)

Brand Name: Entresto

Dual Mechanism:

  • Sacubitril: Inhibits neprilysin → ↑ natriuretic peptides
  • Valsartan: ARB effects
PARADIGM-HF Trial: Showed superiority over enalapril in reducing CV death and HF hospitalization

Switching Protocol:

  1. Stop ACE inhibitor
  2. 36-hour washout period
  3. Start ARNI at low dose

5. Diuretics

Loop Diuretics: Furosemide, Bumetanide, Torsemide

Thiazides: Hydrochlorothiazide, Chlorthalidone (for mild HF)

Clinical Use:

  • Symptom relief (no mortality benefit)
  • Adjust dose to maintain euvolemia
  • Combine with salt restriction
Adverse Effects: Electrolyte disturbances, dehydration, ototoxicity (loops), gout, photosensitivity (thiazides)

6. SGLT2 Inhibitors

Examples: Dapagliflozin, Empagliflozin

Mechanism: Inhibit renal glucose reabsorption → osmotic diuresis, may improve myocardial metabolism

Key Trials:

  • DAPA-HF: Dapagliflozin in HFrEF
  • EMPEROR-Reduced: Empagliflozin in HFrEF
  • EMPEROR-Preserved: Empagliflozin in HFpEF
Advantage: Benefits seen regardless of diabetes status

7. Additional Therapies

Digoxin: Symptom relief in sinus rhythm, no mortality benefit

Ivabradine: For patients with HR >70 on max BB dose

Hydralazine/ISDN: Especially beneficial in African Americans

Device Therapy:

  • ICD for primary/secondary prevention
  • CRT for specific QRS patterns
