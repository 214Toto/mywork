Heart Failure Management - Interactive Roadmap

1. Sulphonamides

Examples:

  1. Nalidixic acid
  2. Ciprofloxacin
  3. Norfloxacin
  4. Ofloxacin
  5. Levofloxacin
  6. Moxifloxacin

Mechanism:

  • Quinolones inhibit the two topoisomerases, DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV.
  • Generally, DNA gyrase is the target of quinolones in gram-negative bacteria, while topoisomerase IV is the primary target in gram-positive organisms.

Indications:

  • Ciprofloxacin:
    • Effective against gram-negative bacteria like enteric gram-negative bacilli, gonococci, chlamydia, Pseudomonas aeruginosa.
    • Used to treat infections like UTI, prostatitis, and PID.
  • Fluoroquinolones:
    • Treat diarrhea infections caused by Campylobacter, Salmonella, Shigella, Yersinia enterocolitica
    • Effective against traveler's diarrhea caused by enterotoxigenic E. coli
    • Used with other drugs to treat intra-abdominal, bone, and skin infections
    • Advanced respiratory fluoroquinolones like levofloxacin, moxifloxacin, gemifloxacin are effective against pneumococci and used in respiratory tract infections (sinusitis, bronchitis, H. influenzae, M. catarrhalis)
    • Effective against community-acquired pneumonia
    • Used in mycobacterial infections including intracellular and multidrug-resistant tuberculosis

Side Effects:

  • Tendinitis and tendon rupture; avoid in children, adolescents, pregnant or nursing women
  • Increased risk of tendon rupture in elderly (>60 years), corticosteroid users, and those with kidney/heart/lung transplants
  • Prolongs QT interval (risk of arrhythmias)
  • Blood sugar alterations (hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia)
  • Seizures
  • Phototoxicity (dermatitis after exposure)
  • Inhibit cytochrome P450 1A2 (affects metabolism of drugs like caffeine, theophylline, etc.)

2. Beta Blockers

Examples: Carvedilol, Bisoprolol, Metoprolol succinate

Mechanism: Block β1-adrenergic receptors → reduce sympathetic overactivity

Important: Start at low dose and titrate slowly in stable patients

Key Points:

  • Only use evidence-based beta blockers in HF
  • May initially worsen symptoms before improvement
  • Contraindicated in acute decompensated HF

3. Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists

Examples: Spironolactone, Eplerenone

Mechanism: Block aldosterone effects → reduce sodium retention and fibrosis

Monitoring Required: Potassium levels (risk of hyperkalemia), renal function

Clinical Use:

  • NYHA Class II-IV with reduced EF
  • Post-MI with reduced EF and symptoms

4. ARNI (Sacubitril/Valsartan)

Brand Name: Entresto

Dual Mechanism:

  • Sacubitril: Inhibits neprilysin → ↑ natriuretic peptides
  • Valsartan: ARB effects
PARADIGM-HF Trial: Showed superiority over enalapril in reducing CV death and HF hospitalization

Switching Protocol:

  1. Stop ACE inhibitor
  2. 36-hour washout period
  3. Start ARNI at low dose

5. Diuretics

Loop Diuretics: Furosemide, Bumetanide, Torsemide

Thiazides: Hydrochlorothiazide, Chlorthalidone (for mild HF)

Clinical Use:

  • Symptom relief (no mortality benefit)
  • Adjust dose to maintain euvolemia
  • Combine with salt restriction
Adverse Effects: Electrolyte disturbances, dehydration, ototoxicity (loops), gout, photosensitivity (thiazides)

6. SGLT2 Inhibitors

Examples: Dapagliflozin, Empagliflozin

Mechanism: Inhibit renal glucose reabsorption → osmotic diuresis, may improve myocardial metabolism

Key Trials:

  • DAPA-HF: Dapagliflozin in HFrEF
  • EMPEROR-Reduced: Empagliflozin in HFrEF
  • EMPEROR-Preserved: Empagliflozin in HFpEF
Advantage: Benefits seen regardless of diabetes status

7. Additional Therapies

Digoxin: Symptom relief in sinus rhythm, no mortality benefit

Ivabradine: For patients with HR >70 on max BB dose

Hydralazine/ISDN: Especially beneficial in African Americans

Device Therapy:

  • ICD for primary/secondary prevention
  • CRT for specific QRS patterns
